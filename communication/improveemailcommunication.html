Improve Email Communication

Address 3 - Ws(Who, What and When) while assigning the task:

When you assign work to anyone through email, then these 3 W's are mandatory to avoid any potential miscommunication. Let us look at each of these Ws in detail:

  1. Who:
    • This is basically the name of the person who you want to complete the task.
    • When you are assigning tasks in an email, you should start the ask by using the name of a single person(not using group)
    • If you assign the task using "All" or "Team", you will trigger Bystander Effect, where everyone will assume someone else will pick the task and no one ever does.

    Incase of trainings which you want whole team to undergo, its okay to address to whole team.

  2. What:

    • This is the description of the exact task you need someone to do.
    • Don't be ambiguous and avoid making any assumptions.

  3. When:

    • This refers to the exact date and time whent he/she taks needs to be completed by.
    • One should always give a deadline, even if it's a fake because it gives your recepient both a clear goal and incentive to get the task done.
    • Include timezone when you have people working across timezones.

Writing Emails With Proper Subject Line:

  1. What is good subject line?:

    • Summarises the email
    • Helps with email retrieval/searching

  2. Tips for creating good subject line:

    • Action oriented emails:

      Include the 3Ws in abbreviated way in subject line if the email includes actions you need someone to do. Also its a good habbit to include [ACTION] prefix in the subject line to stress the importance.
      Example:[ACTION] Micheal - need the audit report by tomorrow EOD

    • Not an action oriented emails:

      Add prefix modifiers such as URGENT, CONFIDENTIAL, IMP or FYI in the subject to explain what the email is about. If modifiers are added as suffix then it might get truncated in mobile apps due to window size.

    • Write entire email if its short message:

      If your message is extremely short, then use the subject line itself as the entire email, just like you would in a text message. Suffix the message with <EOM>, which stands for "End Of Message".
      Example:Quick question: Are you attending the meeting tomorrow?

    • Change the subject line only when needed:

      While replying to email, avoid modifying the subject line unless you absolutely have to. Because most email applications, identify emails based on the subject line text, and group them usnig it. so changing the subject line will mess the grouping feature up.

    • Change the subject line on below scenarios:

      • The topic itself changes: (i.e. the original subject line doesnt apply anymore) In this case, start a new email thread with new subject line and state something like: "Team - I'm moving this conversation to a new email thread so thigns don't get confusing.
      • You're removing recepients from an email:(Due to confidential reason for example) Start a new subjectline by adding a prefix modifier like [Removing Vendor] or [Internal Only] so that everyone's aware.

Write Emails that are 5 sentences or less:

Longer emails are TL(Too Long) & DR (Difficul to Read)

  • Advantages of Shorter emails:

    • Faster replies
    • Less "fluff"
      • Sweet spot: 5 sentences or less.

  • Tips to write shorter emails:

    • Know what you want:

      Most people don't know what they want when they send an email. This is partly due to a form of cognitive bias call Curse of knowledge, where things might be clear in your own head but not translated in right way in text. Reason for sending the email should be 100% clear and you should ask yourself, "What do I really want the recipient to do or to know?" as a first step before writing a single word.

    • Get to the point immediately:

      Get to the point as fast as possible right at the begining of the email. Don't pad your message with useless verbiage. Start with the most important information first.

    • Highlight a lack of required response or action:

      • If you done need or expect a reply to your email, then state that in your message as well.
      • At the beginning of such email, type FYI (For Your Information) or NNTR (No Need To Reply). This would convey clear message to the user the at no action is required.
      • A similar one is "No action needed" where tou dont expect your readers to take action and email is just for the awareness.

Breaking longer emails into 2 parts (1. Quick Summary and 2. Details):

Quick Summary:

This section should be five sentences or less. It should summarize the mail points of your long email. State all actions so that they are not burried in the long email. Think of this quick summary section as an executive summary of a long report - at a glance, the paragraph gives people the gist of your message if they dont have time to read the whole thing.

Details:

Label the section with subheadings like "Details", "Background", or "Supporting Information". This is where tou write out all the additional information that supports your summary. Don't shy away from repeating information that in the quick summary section. In fact, doing so helps to reinforce the points again.

How to make your emails scannable:

Why make emails scannable?

To make your readers focus on the stuff that matters in your messages. This applies to both long and short messages

  • Tip #1: Use Bullet Points:

    • Use billet points for all your actions and questions
    • This will make the (action/question)messages stand out and not buried in paragraphs.
    • When you use 3Ws. every W action should be on seperate bullet points for easy readability.

  • Tip #2: Use subheadings & white space:

    • Use subheadings & white space to break up your text and help your words breather
    • Subheading examples: "Next Steps", "Final Actions", "Meeting Attendees", etc.
    • White space: Break up long paragraphs and include more frequent line spaces within your text.

  • Tip #3: Use highlight and/or bold texts

    • Highlighting and bolding helps to make the important words or phrases to stand out.
    • Eg: Action, names and dates

Show instead of tell by attaching screenshots:

Why attach screenshot?

  • They save time
  • Makes life easier for recepients
  • They are easy to implement
  • They produce potential miscommunication

When to use screenshots?

  • To show someone where to go on a website
  • To give someone step-by-step instruction about a process.
  • To show an error message to help out in troubleshooting

Spell out time zones, dates and Acronyms:

TimeZone:

When action is given to a person working across different time zone then it creates confusion. So its better to spell out the timezone to avoid confusion.

Spell out acronyms:

  • Spell out acronyms at-least once in a message when you are not sure whether everyone in the group knows the acronym.
  • Best practice is to spell the acronym in the first occurrence.

Use "If..Then" statements

  • They reduce the amount of follow up emails, you have to write
  • They clarify the expectations and assumptions so that there is absolutely no confusion for the readers
  • They hold people accountable for getting back to you by a certain date or time

Present options instead of open ended questions:

Don't end an email with questions for generic suggestions;

Dont end an email with phrases like "Thoughts?" or "What do you all think about this?". Instead, invite opinios by stating something like, "Do you think we should do something like A, B, or C?. This guides them give specific answers rather than inviting never-ending opinions.

Don't ask "When do you think we should meet?":

  • When you ask for such input from 10 member thread in different timezones then you would get avalanche of responses
  • A better way to state something like "Can we meet on Monday for 30mins after 2 weeks?"

Re-read your email once for a content check:

Why re-read your emails?

  • Avoiding spelling or grammar mistakes
  • Avoid content mistakes like replying to wrong question, stating the facts incorrectly or adding in wrong date. Content mistakes misrepresents what you want to say and could have worst consequences. 95% of those mistakes can be avoided if you re-read the email at least once before sending

Tips:

  • Re-read your entire email:

    Re-read your entire email before clicking send button. That's because there could be content mistakes due to curse of knowledge. Your knowledge might be clear in your mind, but unless you put yourself in your reader's shoes and re-read the whole email from top-to-bottom, you can't catch those content mistakes. Somethings you want to double check includes names, date, time, timezone and acronyms.

  • Re-read question and answers:

    If you are replying to questions then re-read both the questions and your answers to ensure that your responses makes sense.

Save drafts of repetitive emails:

Create drafts for emails for repetitive emails like report, MOM, etc. So that its easy to retrieve the thread, readers are used to format and it saves time.

How to properly use 'REPLY ALL':

  • Tip #1: Apply the golder rule for reply all:

    Golder Rule: Don't reply all when only the original sender needs to read your message.
    Some phrases that are major offenders of reply all are: "Congrats", "Thank You", "I agree", "Please remove me from mailing list" and "Please stop replying to this thread!"

  • Tip #2: Mention when you are removing members from the reply all list:

    When you remove a subset of recipients from the mail list, then you should clearly state whom you are removing and why.
    Example: "Removing Ram and Ravi so I don't clog their inbox
    State this phrase in the beginning of the email so that it's not missed.

  • Send a follow-up reply email after any offline discussions:

    Summarize any offline discussions and proactively send a follow-up reply all email to keep every on posted about the progress.

  • State what you want the recipients to do:

    Explicitly state what your want your recipients to do.
    If you don't want them to reply all then simply mention that at the end of your email.
    Example: "Please unicast your responses to me & don't reply all."
    If you want themt o reply all then mention that as well.

Reply to question inline:

Replying to question inline helps to improve the readability and eliminates misunderstanding or miscommunication. Use a different color font to highlight the replies. Copy the original question in your message and then reply inline.

Reply Immediately to time sensitive emails:

Replying immediately to time sensitive emails acknowledges that your got the message and helps to communicate the status to sender. Don't acknowledge in one work, instead add more information to your acknowledgement. As sender if you need the acknowledgement then request the same in email.

Set up Out-Of-Office Emails:

When you're taking time offs, its important to set up OOO emails as auto-reply so that sender knows when will you be able to reply to the message. A perfect OOO email should contain below items:
  • exact period for which you're unavailable
  • How often you might check the messages?
  • Whom to reach out to till you become fully available
  • What to do incase of emergencies
